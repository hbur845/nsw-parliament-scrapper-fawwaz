Summary Offences and Other Legislation Amendment (Public Assemblies) Bill 2025

Second Reading Speech

Debate resumed from 13 November 2025.

Mr ALISTER HENSKENS (Wahroonga) (10:20:19): I resume my second reading speech on the Summary Offences and Other Legislation Amendment (Public Assemblies) Bill 2025. As I started to say on the last occasion, the bill deals with some of the less satisfactory events that have taken place in our community in recent times. Two weekends ago, for the second time since July, we saw a Nazi protest outside of Parliament and Nazis using the symbol of our democracy as a sort of vindication for their vile views, which divide communities, set citizen against citizen and seek to dismantle one of the great multicultural experiments of the world, as we have been able achieve in New South Wales and more broadly in Australia.

We all know the statistics: Well over 50 per cent of the population of New South Wales either was born overseas or has one at least parent born overseas. We have an incredibly diverse community. To allow thugs—there is no other word for them—to lawfully roam around our streets causing social division is not what people in the State want. My bill will give the police and the courts power to make the kind of protests that we saw outside of Parliament unlawful and therefore make them not occur. I believe, and the Coalition believes, that is in the best interest of the New South Wales public.

The bill has been rejected on a number of occasions by the Labor Party, The Greens and the Independents in this Chamber. If this reintroduced bill passes through Parliament, it will give power to the police and the courts to stop extremists protesting in our streets, like the Nazis did outside our Parliament, and appropriating our national symbols—like the Opera House, the Harbour Bridge and Parliament House—for their undesirable causes. If the bill passes through Parliament, it will also allow the courts, subject to the constitutional implied right of freedom of political communication, to say to potential protesters, "Your group has already protested three times this year, at great cost to the taxpayer in terms of policing and the diversion of resources away from things like domestic violence, youth crime and other things that impact our community. If you want to lawfully hold more than three protests, then conditions will be placed on you so that you pay for some or all of the policing costs and other costs that arise."

It is not mandatory that that be imposed. All of the circumstances of the case can be looked at. If the three previous protests had been five people outside Parliament House, with no diversion of policing resources, then they do not need to expend policing resources on the fourth one. But in the past couple of years, policing these protests has cost the taxpayer tens of millions of dollars. In the circumstance of a massive protest, the bill will make it possible for some of those costs to be borne by the protesters and the organisers of those protests, rather than the taxpayer, as a condition of them getting approval for more protests. We believe that is a commonsense proposal. The Premier actually floated an idea that there should be some recovery. Unfortunately, the Premier, the Labor Party, The Greens and the Independents did not support that when I brought a bill before the Parliament in the past; I am now reintroducing that through this bill.

We have seen that these extremist groups like to hide their faces. They do not like to take personal responsibility for the vile ideology that they are spewing in our community. Another thing the bill will do is give the police the power to pull out the protesters who are disguising their appearance, unless it is for good religious reasons; they can go back to the protest only if they remove their face coverings and stop hiding their identity. Modern technologies of photography and film assists police in the identification of people doing illegal activities within a protest. That is thwarted if people are allowed to hide their appearance. Therefore, if these groups want to continue to lawfully protest, then they need to take responsibility for their actions and not hide their appearance.

Those are the three key measures within the bill—all quite reasonable, one would think. I guess the rhetorical questions are these. Why have the Premier, the Labor Party, The Greens and the Independents voted against these commonsense measures on more than one occasion this year? Why did they force my last bill to lapse so that I have to reintroduce it and give another second reading speech? I think they are legitimate questions that the public should be asking, keeping in mind the provisions in my bill. For the benefit of members, I will go through the details of my bill so that they can read it inHansard—or maybe they are listening in their offices—and understand what the bill provides. At the moment we have a regime that does not force police officers on the ground to make difficult legal determinations about the lawfulness of a public assembly as it is happening.

The process under the Summary Offences Act is that, more than seven days before a protest, the organisers need to go to the police to seek a form 1, which is an approval for the protest if it is conducted in accordance with the time, the route and other conditions that may be imposed by police. If the protest is conducted in accordance with those conditions then it is deemed a lawful protest. If organisers are knocked back because, for example, the police are unsatisfied with the protest or it is less than seven days beforehand, there is a provision to apply to the Supreme Court for a judicial determination on whether the protest should be allowed. At the moment, when it goes to court, the argument is essentially only around whether the proposal for the protest would promote public safety. No consideration is given to other aspects of public interest. I want to set out a non-exhaustive list of things that the court and the police must take into account before they deem a protest to be a lawful protest, in advance of it taking place.

The things I want considered—in addition to public safety, which is still number one—include the impact of the public assembly on public amenity and convenience. It is obviously not furthering public amenity and convenience to have Nazis parading around in the streets within our community. That would be an aspect that the courts could take into account, amongst other aspects of public amenity and convenience. Considerations would also include the adverse impacts of the public assembly on the economy, including businesses. For example, if repeated protests every Saturday clog up the city and stop people coming to do their shopping and help the economy of New South Wales, or if the proposed protest would clash with a sporting event or a concert—
